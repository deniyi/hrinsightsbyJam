The Precarity Allowance Across All 17 OHADA Member States: A Complete Overview

A country-by-country map of an entitlement millions of fixed-term workers deserve to know about — and too many employers still overlook.

By Jamiu Adekanmbi  ·  Doctoral Candidate / HR Leader  ·  OHADA Zone  ·  Extended Edition

A fixed-term employee's contract ends in Abidjan, Dakar, Kinshasa, or Niamey. The HR team issues the final payslip. He signs the settlement document, and that's that. But one question quietly goes unasked: did he receive everything he was legally owed? In too many cases, the answer is no — and the missing line item is called the indemnité de précarité, or precarity allowance. Here is the complete picture across all 17 OHADA member states.

For many HR professionals in the OHADA zone, this term calls to mind French labour law. That is a geographical and legal mistake. The precarity allowance — also known as the end-of-contract indemnity — exists in the Labour Codes of francophone Africa in various forms and at varying rates. Its underlying logic, however, is universal: to compensate the worker for the inherent instability of fixed-term employment.

What the Precarity Allowance Is

When an employee accepts a fixed-term contract (CDD — contrat à durée déterminée), they are, at least temporarily, giving up the security of a permanent position. When that contract ends and no permanent role follows, the law recognises a financial compensation for having borne that instability. It is a wage supplement, calculated on the total gross remuneration earned over the life of the contract and paid at the point of final settlement.

It is distinct from notice pay, severance indemnity, and accrued holiday pay. It is a right unique to the condition of the fixed-term worker.

"This is not a favour granted to the employee. It is the legal counterpart of the risk they agreed to carry."

A Critical Reminder: OHADA Does Not (Yet) Govern Labour Law

OHADA harmonises business law, not labour law. A Uniform Act on Labour Law has been in development for years, but as of today, each member state retains its own national Labour Code. The rules on the precarity allowance therefore differ significantly from one country to the next — which is precisely what this overview sets out to clarify.

What This Table Reveals

Only four countries provide an explicit, directly applicable statutory rate: the DRC (5%), Côte d'Ivoire (3%), Guinea Conakry (5%), and Senegal (7%). These are the markets where the obligation is clearest and where the risk of litigation for non-payment is most immediate.

In five other countries — Mali, Niger, Togo, Chad, and the Central African Republic — the entitlement is recognised in principle but its rate is deferred to collective agreements or implementing decrees. This means the applicable sectoral collective agreement may provide stronger protection than the statute's silence, and employers must consult it before closing out any fixed-term contract.

The remaining eight countries — Benin, Burkina Faso, Cameroon, Comoros, Congo-Brazzaville, Gabon, Guinea-Bissau — present either statutory silence or an express provision for no indemnity at normal term. A critical caveat: the absence of a statutory rate does not necessarily mean no sectoral collective agreement fills the gap.

⚠ Critical Point: The Collective Agreement Overrides Statutory Silence In countries where the Labour Code is silent on the precarity allowance, a sectoral collective agreement may very well provide for it — and it will then be binding on the employer. Failing to consult the collective agreement applicable to your sector is a compliance error that labour tribunals penalise with increasing regularity.

Grey Areas Common Across the Entire Zone

Renewed fixed-term contracts: In countries where the allowance is owed, it is calculated on the total duration of the precarious employment relationship, including all renewals. It is not calculated per individual contract, but across the entire period.

Conversion to a permanent contract: Wherever the allowance is provided for, it is not owed if the fixed-term contract leads directly to a permanent CDI for an equivalent role. The precarity ends; the compensation for it ends too.

Early termination: Where the worker terminates the contract early, or where termination follows from their own serious misconduct, the allowance is generally not owed. Where the employer terminates without valid cause, the allowance remains owed alongside any damages.

The internship loophole: The use of internship agreements as a substitute for fixed-term contracts — sidestepping end-of-contract obligations entirely — is a growing reclassification risk across the OHADA zone wherever genuine subordinate work is performed.

Why This Goes Beyond Compliance

In economies where inflation regularly erodes household purchasing power, the fixed-term worker bears the market's instability alone, on behalf of the business. The precarity allowance is labour law's chosen response to rebalance that burden. Failing to pay it is not a saving — it is a deferred liability, with interest that is financial, legal, and reputational.

What You Should Review Today

  • Identify which country each of your fixed-term contracts is executed in — the applicable rules are those of the country of execution, not the company's registered seat.
  • Consult both the applicable national Labour Code AND the sectoral collective agreement for your industry.
  • For countries with an explicit rate (DRC, Côte d'Ivoire, Guinea, Senegal), integrate the allowance into your payroll system from the moment the CDD is signed.
  • For countries with a deferred rate (Mali, Niger, Togo, Chad, CAR), ask your legal counsel to confirm the applicable rate via the relevant collective agreement.
  • For silent countries (Benin, Burkina, Cameroon, Gabon, etc.), verify whether the sectoral collective agreement fills the statutory gap.
  • Do your CDD templates include a clause on the end-of-contract indemnity consistent with local legislation?
  • Does your offboarding process include a systematic check before the final settlement document is issued?
